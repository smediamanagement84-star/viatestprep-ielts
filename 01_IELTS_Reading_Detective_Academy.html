Skim — Build the Map

2–3 minutes max

Skimming is not speed reading. It's strategic glancing. You read the title, the first paragraph carefully, the first sentence of every other paragraph, and the last paragraph. Goal: build a mental map of where different topics live — not understand them yet.

Goal: "Para A = history. Para B = problems. Para C = a Japanese study." That's it. Now you know where to hunt.
Quick drill — Tap what to skim
🔦

Scan — The Searchlight

Hunt for a single keyword

Scanning means letting your eyes fly across the text, ignoring full sentences, until you hit a keyword (or its synonym). When you find it — stop. Then read carefully around it for the answer.

Best keywords: proper nouns, dates, numbers, technical terms, italics — anything hard to paraphrase.

The Lost Library of Sutton

For more than a century, the small market town of Sutton in northern England was famous for an unlikely treasure: a private library assembled by the eccentric collector Hugh Marlow between 1893 and 1925. Marlow's collection, eventually numbering over 12,000 volumes, was renowned for its medieval manuscripts and early scientific texts.

The library's fame, however, was matched by its inaccessibility. Marlow rarely allowed visitors and refused to lend any item to outside scholars. After his death in 1928, the collection passed to a local trust, which opened the building to the public for limited hours but did little to catalogue or conserve its contents.

By the late 1970s, water damage and theft had taken a serious toll. A 1981 audit, led by the historian Dr. Eleanor Ash, revealed that as many as 3,000 volumes were missing or damaged beyond repair. Critics blamed inadequate funding, while the trust argued the building's location had always made conservation difficult.

In 2002, a digitisation project began. Although progress was initially slow, by 2018 over 8,000 volumes had been scanned and made publicly available online. The project drew funding from three universities and a private foundation. The lead archivist credited the project's success to the diversity of expertise on the team rather than to any single innovation.

⑦ The Mistake Journal

This is what separates the 8s from the 7s. Every wrong answer in practice gets logged with why it was wrong. Use this template every session.

1 · The Question

Copy the question and the question type (T/F/NG, MCQ, etc.).

2 · My Answer / Correct

Side-by-side. Don't let yourself round it down to "silly mistake".

3 · Why I Got It Wrong

Misread question? Distractor trap? Missed a synonym? Used my own knowledge?

4 · The Rule

One-line rule that would have saved you. Re-read it before next practice.

Worked example — click to open
Q: "The 1981 audit was led by Dr. Patel." (T/F/NG)
My answer: NOT GIVEN · Correct: FALSE
Why wrong: The passage names the auditor as Dr. Ash. I saw the year 1981 and a name and assumed the question was just "extra detail". I missed that the passage actively contradicts the statement.
Rule: If the passage actively corrects the statement → FALSE. If it simply doesn't mention it → NOT GIVEN.
