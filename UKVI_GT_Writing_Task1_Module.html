MODULE 03 · WRITING TASK 1 · UKVI GENERAL TRAINING

You are not describing a graph.
You are writing a letter.

IELTS GT Writing Task 1 is entirely different from Academic. No graphs. No charts. Just a letter — formal, semi-formal, or informal — in 20 minutes, 150+ words. This is where your Skilled Worker visa application starts. Get the tone wrong and lose marks immediately.

⏱ 20 min · Task 1 ✉ 150+ words 🎯 Band 7+ target 🇬🇧 UKVI Skilled Worker 3 bullet points · 3 paragraphs

② GT Task 1 vs Academic Task 1 — A Complete Swap

This is not a minor adjustment. The entire task changes. Here's what your students need to unlearn.

❌ Academic Task 1 (NOT for UKVI GT) Describe a graph, chart, table, pie chart, or diagram. Report data. No opinions. Formal register only. Paraphrase the title. Write an overview.
✅ GT Task 1 (UKVI Skilled Worker) Write a letter. You are given 3 bullet points — address all three. Choose your tone based on who you're writing to. You CAN use "I". Sign off correctly.
📐 The Spec (same timing, different task) 20 minutes · Minimum 150 words · 3 bullet points in the prompt, each must become a paragraph · Tone determined by recipient · Task 2 still worth double — do Task 2 first.
⚠ The #1 Mistake GT Students Make Writing a formal letter when the prompt calls for informal — or vice versa. Tone mismatch is a Task Achievement failure. Identify the register in the first 30 seconds. It's always signalled in the prompt.

③ The 3 Letter Types — Blueprints & Worked Examples

Every GT Task 1 prompt is one of three types. Tap each to open the full blueprint, tone guide, and a worked example relevant to Skilled Worker visa scenarios.

🏛

Formal Letter

Official body, unknown recipient

🏢

Semi-Formal Letter

Manager, landlord, colleague

💬

Informal Letter

Friend, family member

🏛 When is it formal? Writing to: a government office, a council, a utility company, a hospital, a company (general), an employer you don't know, a university. Prompt signals: "write to the manager of…", "write to the local council", "write to the company".
SALUTATION
"Dear Sir or Madam," — use when name unknown. "Dear Mr/Ms [Name]," — use only if the prompt gives a name.
OPENING LINE
State your purpose immediately. "I am writing to…" (complain / request / enquire about / draw attention to). Never start with "I received your…" without context.
BODY ×3
One paragraph per bullet point. Each paragraph: topic sentence → detail → link or consequence. Never mix bullets in one paragraph.
CLOSING LINE
"I look forward to hearing from you." or "I trust this matter will be resolved promptly."
SIGN-OFF
"Yours faithfully," — always when you used "Dear Sir or Madam". "Yours sincerely," — only when you used the person's name.

📋 Worked Example — Skilled Worker Scenario

Prompt: You recently moved to a new city for work and are having problems with your rental flat. Write a letter to your landlord in which you:
• explain what the problems are
• describe how these problems are affecting you
• say what you would like the landlord to do
Dear Sir or Madam, I am writing to bring to your attention several ongoing issues with the property at 14B Hanover Road, which I have been renting since 1 April of this year. The most pressing problem is a significant damp patch on the bedroom ceiling, which has expanded considerably over the past three weeks. Additionally, the boiler has been producing only intermittent hot water since I moved in, and two of the kitchen cupboards have broken hinges that prevent them from closing securely. These issues are having a direct impact on my daily life. The damp has already damaged some of my clothing, and the unreliable hot water supply makes it difficult for me to prepare adequately for work each morning. As a result, I have been unable to fully settle into the property. I would respectfully request that a qualified maintenance team be arranged to inspect and repair all three issues within the next ten working days. I would also appreciate confirmation of any proposed dates so that I can make myself available. I look forward to hearing from you. Yours faithfully, A. Patel

Word count: ~185 · All 3 bullets covered · Formal register maintained · Correct sign-off ("Yours faithfully" because "Dear Sir or Madam" was used)

🏢 When is it semi-formal? Writing to: your manager, HR department, a landlord you know, a colleague in a senior position, a doctor. Prompt signals: "write to your manager", "write to your supervisor", "write to your employer". Most Skilled Worker visa scenarios fall here.
SALUTATION
"Dear Mr/Ms [Name]," — if prompt gives a name. "Dear [First Name]," — only if you are clearly on first-name terms. When in doubt, use title + surname.
OPENING LINE
A brief context-setter. "I am writing regarding…" or "I wanted to bring to your attention…". Warmer than formal but still professional.
BODY ×3
One paragraph per bullet. More natural flow than formal — you can use contractions sparingly (I've, I'd). Keep professional vocabulary.
CLOSING LINE
"I would appreciate your guidance on this matter." or "Please let me know if you require any further information."
SIGN-OFF
"Kind regards," or "Best regards," — both appropriate. Avoid "Yours faithfully/sincerely" — too stiff for semi-formal.

📋 Worked Example — Skilled Worker Scenario

Prompt: You have recently started a new job in the UK and would like to request some changes to your working arrangement. Write a letter to your manager in which you:
• explain why you are making the request
• describe the changes you would like
• explain how these changes will benefit both you and the company
Dear Ms Johnson, I am writing to request a discussion about a potential adjustment to my current working arrangement, which I hope you will consider. As you are aware, I relocated from abroad specifically for this role and am still in the process of settling into the area. My daughter's school hours end at 3:30pm, and I am currently relying on costly emergency childcare while I identify a longer-term solution. This situation is placing a considerable strain on my family finances during an already challenging transition. I would like to request a shift in my working hours to 7:30am–3:30pm, four days per week, with the fifth day worked remotely from home. I am confident I can maintain my full productivity under this arrangement, as the majority of my work is carried out independently. I believe this adjustment would benefit the team by ensuring my focus remains fully on my work responsibilities. I am happy to trial this arrangement for an agreed period and review it together with you thereafter. Please let me know if you would be open to meeting to discuss this further. Kind regards, Tariq Hussain

Word count: ~195 · Warm but professional tone · All 3 bullets addressed · "Kind regards" appropriate for semi-formal

💬 When is it informal? Writing to: a friend, a family member, a neighbour you know well. Prompt signals: "write to a friend", "write to your cousin", "write to a friend who is planning to visit". Informal is less common in Skilled Worker scenarios but does appear.
SALUTATION
"Dear [First Name]," always. E.g., "Dear Sarah," — never use a surname in an informal letter.
OPENING LINE
A friendly opener. "How are you? I hope everything is going well." or "It was so good to hear from you!" — natural and warm.
BODY ×3
Conversational. Contractions fine (I've, we're, that's). Can use colloquial phrases, rhetorical questions. Still one bullet per paragraph — don't miss any.
CLOSING LINE
"Anyway, do write back soon!" or "Looking forward to catching up." Friendly and natural.
SIGN-OFF
"Lots of love," / "Best wishes," / "Take care," — pick one that suits the relationship. Never "Yours faithfully/sincerely".

📋 Worked Example — Friend Scenario

Prompt: A friend from your home country is planning to visit you in the UK, where you now live and work. Write a letter in which you:
• explain why you are pleased they are coming
• suggest some things you could do together
• give some advice about what to bring or prepare for
Dear Priya, I can't tell you how excited I was when I got your message! It's been such a long time and I've really missed having you around, so the news that you're finally coming over has honestly made my week. There's so much I want us to do while you're here. I'm thinking we could spend a day in London — maybe the South Bank, Borough Market, and a boat ride on the Thames. If the weather holds up, I'd also love to take a day trip to the Cotswolds, which is absolutely stunning. And of course, we absolutely have to find a proper Sunday roast somewhere! One thing I should warn you about — the weather in England is… unpredictable, to put it kindly! Pack layers, a waterproof jacket, and comfortable walking shoes. Also, if you're planning to use your phone, it might be worth getting a UK SIM card at the airport; it'll save you a lot on roaming charges. Anyway, I'm counting down the days already! Do let me know your final dates as soon as you can so I can book some time off work. Lots of love, Meena

Word count: ~210 · Warm, conversational tone · Contractions used naturally · All 3 bullets covered · "Lots of love" appropriate for close friend

④ The Letter Phrase Bank — Skilled Worker Edition

Ready-to-use phrases organised by function. Memorise one set per category. Flexibility beats memorising whole templates.

🏛 Formal Openings

I am writing to draw your attention to…
I am writing with regard to…
I am writing to enquire about…
I wish to formally complain about…

🏢 Semi-Formal Openings

I wanted to bring to your attention…
I am writing regarding a matter I'd like your advice on…
I hope you don't mind me getting in touch about…
I'm writing to let you know about a change I'd like to discuss…

💬 Informal Openings

How are you? It feels like ages since we last spoke!
I was so happy to get your message!
I hope everything is going well with you and the family.
Sorry I've been a bit out of touch lately — things have been so busy!

🔗 Paragraph Transitions

Regarding the second matter… (formal)
In addition to this… (all registers)
On another note… (semi/informal)
There is also the question of… (formal)

📢 Making Requests

I would be grateful if you could… (formal)
I would appreciate it if… (semi-formal)
Could you possibly…? (semi-formal)
Would you be able to…? (all registers)

🏁 Closings by Register

I look forward to your prompt response. (formal)
Please don't hesitate to contact me if you need anything further. (semi)
Anyway, write back soon — I miss you! (informal)
I trust this matter will receive your urgent attention. (formal)
⚡ The Sign-off Rule — Never Get This Wrong Dear Sir or Madam → Yours faithfully · Dear Mr/Ms [Name] → Yours sincerely · Dear [First Name] → Best wishes / Kind regards / Lots of love

DRILL C · SPOT THE TONE ERROR · +10 XP

Find the sentence that doesn't match the register

This is a formal letter to an HR department. One sentence is wrong. Find it.

Dear Sir or Madam,

I am writing to formally request a reference letter in support of my application for a position at Hartwell Engineering.

I worked in your organisation's accounts department from March 2022 to November 2024. During this period, I was responsible for monthly financial reporting and managed a team of three junior analysts. My performance reviews during this time were consistently rated as 'exceeding expectations'.

Hey, it would be really awesome if you could write me a reference by next Friday if possible? 😊

I look forward to your response and thank you in advance for your time and consideration.

Yours faithfully,
K. Okafor

📐 Band Descriptor Self-Check

After writing, rate yourself honestly on each criterion. This is how your examiner will mark you.

Task Achievement

Did you cover ALL 3 bullet points? Is the register consistent throughout? Did you write 150+ words?

Coherence & Cohesion

Is each bullet point a separate paragraph? Did you use linking phrases between paragraphs?

Lexical Resource

Did you use professional vocabulary? Did you avoid repeating the same words? Appropriate collocations?

Grammar & Register

Did you use the correct sign-off for semi-formal? Any grammar errors you noticed? Mix of sentence types?

📋 Model Answer Reference — Key Points This Letter Should Hit Register: Semi-formal (HR manager) · Salutation: "Dear Ms/Mr [Name]" · Sign-off: "Kind regards" or "Best regards" · Bullet 1: Personal difficulty (e.g., childcare, longer commute, additional cost) · Bullet 2: Work impact (e.g., fatigue, reduced productivity, morale) · Bullet 3: Solutions (e.g., hybrid working, flexible hours, transport allowance) · Never use "Dear Sir or Madam" — you know the department you're writing to.

⑦ Your 7-Day Action Plan

GT Task 1 rewards practice in all three registers. Rotate — don't just drill formal.

DAY 1

Formal only — 2 complaint letters. Focus: "Yours faithfully" trigger + precise vocabulary

DAY 2

Semi-formal — 2 request letters to a manager or HR. Focus: tone balance + bullet coverage

DAY 3

Informal — 1 letter to a friend. Focus: contractions, rhetorical questions, warm closings

DAY 4

Register drill — 10 prompts, classify only. No writing. Focus: identifying tone in 30 seconds

DAY 5

Opening & closing sentences only — write 3 sets of each for all 3 registers. Memorise patterns.

DAY 6

Full timed Task 1 + Task 2 mock — 20 min letter, then 40 min essay. Simulate real conditions.

DAY 7

Peer review session — swap letters with another student. Check: all 3 bullets, register, sign-off.

🇬🇧 UKVI Reminder — Task 2 Still Matters Task 1 (this letter) is worth 1/3 of your Writing score. Task 2 (the essay) is worth 2/3. Always write Task 2 first. If you run out of time, Task 1 is recoverable. Task 2 is not.
