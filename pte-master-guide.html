Top Student Mistakes
Going silent mid-speaking task
Speaking
▾
The AI detects silence as a fluency break and cuts the microphone after 3 seconds. If you lose your place, use "as I was saying" or "moving on to" and keep speaking — never stop.
Selecting too many answers in MCQ Multiple
Reading + Listening — Negative marking applies
▾
Every wrong option selected deducts a point. If you are uncertain about an option, DO NOT select it — leaving it blank scores 0, which is better than a deduction of −1.
Using word-for-word memorised templates
Speaking + Writing
▾
Pearson's AI is trained to detect and penalise robotic memorised responses. Use templates as a structural framework — vary your vocabulary and sentence structures every time.
Ignoring spelling in written tasks
Writing — scored as a separate enabling skill
▾
Spelling is scored independently. Every misspelled word in Essay, Summarise Text, Write Dictation and Listening FiB reduces your Spelling sub-score. Choose British or American English and stay consistent throughout.
Not reading options before audio plays
All Listening tasks
▾
Audio plays ONCE — no replay, no pause. Your preparation time before each recording is the most valuable window you have. Use every second to read the question and all answer options so you know exactly what to listen for.

Speaking
5 task types · Part 1 · Templates, strategies & what to avoid
6–7 items30–40 sec eachScores: Speaking + ReadingHighest value task

A text of up to 60 words appears on screen. After a countdown and beep you read it aloud. This is the single most impactful task in PTE — it scores Speaking (fluency, pronunciation) AND Reading (accuracy) simultaneously.

What The AI Scores
SUB-SKILL
WHAT IT MEASURES
Content
Percentage of words read correctly and in order
Oral Fluency
Natural rhythm, smooth chunked phrasing — no hesitations or false starts
Pronunciation
Correct sound production, word stress, and connected speech
How To Attempt — Step By Step
1
Use all prep time wisely
Skim the full text once. Identify unfamiliar words and rehearse them silently. Mark natural pause points at commas and full stops.
2
Begin immediately after the beep
Start within 1 second. Dead air at the beginning registers as a fluency issue.
3
Read in meaning chunks — not word by word
Group words into phrases: "The government / has announced / new investment policies / for rural development." Pausing at punctuation creates natural rhythm.
4
Stress content words, reduce function words
Emphasise nouns, main verbs, adjectives. Reduce articles (a, the), prepositions (of, in). This is how native English rhythm works — the AI recognises it.
5
Never stop or self-correct
If you mispronounce a word, keep going. Self-correction destroys fluency more than the original error.
6
Finish with 3–5 seconds to spare
Never let the timer cut you off mid-sentence. Aim to finish just before it ends.
Chunking Template
READ IN MEANING CHUNKS — not word by word:
✗ Wrong: "The / risk / of / flooding / in / coastal / areas / is / increasing."

✓ Correct: The risk of flooding / in coastal areas / is increasing / at an alarming rate.

Pause rule: Comma = brief pause (0.3s) · Full stop = longer pause (0.5s)
Stress rule: Give extra volume + length to the main noun in each chunk:
"The RISK of flooding / in COASTal areas / is inCREASing."
Pronunciation Rules For 79+
KEY RULES
▸
Linking: Connect words — "turn_it_on", "an_apple", "at_all". Choppy word-by-word drops fluency.
▸
Schwa: Unstressed vowels reduce to /ə/ — "about" = /əˈbaʊt/, "the" before consonant = /ðə/.
▸
No added vowels: "film" not "fi-lum", "asked" not "ask-ed", "world" not "wor-uld".
▸
Syllable stress: pho-TOG-ra-phy · e-CON-o-my · gov-ERN-ment · en-VI-ron-ment.
▸
Final consonants: "worked" = /wɜːkt/, "watched" = /wɒtʃt/. Never drop them.
What To Avoid
COMMON MISTAKES THAT DROP YOUR SCORE
✗
Going silent mid-text — the AI cuts the microphone after 3 seconds of silence.
✗
Repeating or correcting yourself — self-correction destroys fluency even if the word was wrong.
✗
Adding words not in the text — Content score is based on exact matches only.
✗
Skipping words or lines — each skipped word reduces your Content score.
✗
Monotone delivery — vary pitch on stressed syllables for natural prosody.
✗
Speaking too quietly — microphone may not register low-volume speech accurately.
10–12 items15 sec to respondScores: Speaking + Listening2nd highest value task

An audio sentence plays once. You must repeat it exactly — same words, same order, same rhythm. With 10–12 items it is the most frequently occurring task in the test, and simultaneously scores Speaking and Listening.

What The AI Scores
SUB-SKILL
WHAT IT MEASURES
Content
Percentage of original words reproduced correctly and in the right order
Oral Fluency
Smooth, uninterrupted delivery at natural pace — no choppy hesitation
Pronunciation
Correct sounds and stress patterns matching the original speaker
How To Attempt — Step By Step
1
Listen without writing or typing
Full attention on audio only. Writing shorthand means you will miss the second half of the sentence while writing the first.
2
Use the 4-Block Memory Method
As the sentence plays: ANCHOR the first 2–3 words → CAPTURE the subject + verb core → HOLD the last 3–4 words → RECONSTRUCT in order.
3
Speak immediately after audio ends
Begin within 1 second. The microphone activates automatically. Pausing longer than 2 seconds costs fluency marks.
4
Mirror the speaker's rhythm and stress
The AI compares your prosody to the original speaker. Match their pace and stress patterns — not just the words.
5
If you miss a word, substitute a synonym
Insert a grammatically correct synonym rather than going silent. "rapidly growing" instead of "rapidly expanding" — partial credit beats silence.
The 4-Block Memory Template
Example sentence: "The government has allocated significant funding for renewable energy development."

BLOCK 1 — Anchor (first 2–3 words): The government has
BLOCK 2 — Core (subject + verb): allocated significant funding
BLOCK 3 — Tail (last 3–4 words): renewable energy development
BLOCK 4 — Reconstruct: The government has → allocated significant funding → for renewable energy development.
MEMORY TIPS
▸
Short sentences (under 8 words): aim for 100% word-perfect reproduction.
▸
Long sentences (12+ words): prioritise the first half and the last 3–4 words — the middle is easiest to recover.
▸
Never say "um", "uh" or "I don't know" — these register as fluency breaks.
▸
Match the original speaker's intonation on stressed words — the AI measures prosody, not just words.
▸
Practice daily with dictation tools: Google Dictation, ELSA Speak, or Otter.ai.
What To Avoid
SCORE-KILLERS
✗
Writing during the audio — you will miss the second half of the sentence.
✗
Changing word order even if all words are correct — order is part of the score.
✗
Pausing longer than 2 seconds before speaking — the AI scores the silence as dysfluency.
✗
Adding extra words not in the original sentence.
✗
Repeating part of the sentence to buy time — registers as dysfluency.
3–4 items25 sec prep · 40 sec speakScores: Speaking

An image appears — bar chart, line graph, pie chart, table, map, process diagram, or photo. You have 25 seconds to study it then 40 seconds to describe it. You must fill the full 40 seconds without stopping.

What The AI Scores
SUB-SKILL
WHAT IT MEASURES
Content
Did you cover the key features, overall trend, and speak for the full time?
Oral Fluency
Natural rhythm — no dead silence, smooth transitions between points
Pronunciation
Clear, intelligible delivery of data points and descriptive vocabulary
How To Attempt — Step By Step
1
During prep: build a 5-sentence skeleton on notepad
Write: ① What is it? ② Overall trend ③ Highest value ④ Lowest / contrast ⑤ Conclusion. 5 sentences × 8 seconds = 40 seconds perfectly filled.
2
Always start with introduction — never with data
Open by stating what the image shows. "The bar chart illustrates..." This signals to the AI you understood the visual first.
3
State the overall trend as your second sentence
The Overview is the most important sentence. Always give a general summary before specific data points.
4
Select 2–3 key data points — not all of them
Trying to mention every data point causes rushed monotone delivery. Pick highest, lowest, and one interesting comparison.
5
Use "approximately" for unclear values
Never invent precise numbers. "approximately 45%" and "roughly half" are correct. The AI does not penalise approximation.
6
Always end with a conclusion sentence
If time is running short: "Overall, this data highlights a clear pattern of [X]." Never stop mid-thought.
Universal 5-Sentence Template
① INTRODUCTION (5–6 sec):
"The [image type: bar chart / line graph / pie chart / table / map / diagram] illustrates [main topic] [time period / location if shown]."

② OVERVIEW — Key Trend (7–8 sec):
"Overall, it is evident that [most striking finding or dominant pattern]."

③ HIGHEST / MOST PROMINENT (8 sec):
"Notably, [category with highest value] recorded the highest figure at approximately [value], [followed by / compared with / while] [contrast detail]."

④ LOWEST / CONTRAST (7–8 sec):
"In contrast, [lowest category] showed the smallest figure at [value], [additional observation]."

⑤ CONCLUSION (6–7 sec):
"In conclusion, the [image type] clearly demonstrates that [key takeaway — link to real-world implication]."
Vocabulary Banks
INCREASES
rose sharply · surged to · climbed steadily · increased dramatically · peaked at · reached a high of
DECREASES
declined sharply · fell dramatically · dropped to · plummeted · hit a low of · decreased steadily
STABLE
remained stable · levelled off · fluctuated around · showed little change · stayed relatively constant
COMPARISON
in contrast · compared with · whereas · while · on the other hand · significantly higher than
PROCESS
first · then · subsequently · after which · finally · the process begins with · this leads to
MAP
was demolished · was replaced by · was built · was extended · to the north/south of · in the centre
What To Avoid
MISTAKES
✗
Going silent — if you run out of content, repeat the overview in different words to fill time.
✗
Describing every single data point — causes rushed monotone delivery that drops fluency.
✗
Inventing precise numbers not visible in the image — use "approximately" for unclear values.
✗
Using past tense for process diagrams — always present simple passive: "water IS filtered".
✗
Memorising one fixed script word-for-word — the AI detects and penalises repetitive memorised responses.
✗
Reading numbers too fast — "forty-five percent" should take a full second; rush it and it sounds like gibberish.
2–3 itemsAudio up to 90 sec10 sec prep · 40 sec speakScores: Speaking + Listening

An audio lecture plays — sometimes with a still image. After it ends you have 10 seconds to prepare then 40 seconds to summarise it in your own words. Focus on: main topic, central argument, and 2–3 supporting points.

What The AI Scores
SUB-SKILL
WHAT IT MEASURES
Content
Did you accurately capture the main topic, central claim, and key supporting points?
Oral Fluency
Smooth connected delivery — no long pauses or choppy word-by-word output
Pronunciation
Clear production of English sounds and natural word stress
Note-Taking System During Audio
WHAT TO WRITE ON YOUR NOTEPAD
▸
First 10 sec: Write the TOPIC — 1–2 words only. Always stated early.
▸
Middle section: Note key nouns, numbers, names, technical terms. Hardest to recall from memory.
▸
Last 10 sec: Note the conclusion or recommendation — speakers always signal it at the end.
▸
Use symbols: → = leads to · + = also · ↑ = increase · ↓ = decrease · ? = question raised.
▸
Write KEYWORDS only — do NOT attempt full sentences. Your listening will suffer.
5-Sentence Response Template
① TOPIC (5 sec):
"The lecture discusses [main topic]."

② MAIN ARGUMENT (8 sec):
"The speaker argues that [central claim], explaining that [main reasoning]."

③ SUPPORTING DETAIL 1 (8 sec):
"Furthermore, [first key supporting point or evidence], which [significance or implication]."

④ SUPPORTING DETAIL 2 (8 sec):
"The speaker also mentions that [second key point], highlighting [why it matters]."

⑤ CONCLUSION (7 sec):
"In conclusion, the lecture highlights the importance of [key takeaway]."
SAMPLE RESPONSE (~40 seconds)
The lecture discusses the psychological effects of social media on teenagers. The speaker argues that excessive screen time is directly linked to rising anxiety levels, explaining that algorithmic design deliberately extends engagement beyond healthy limits. Furthermore, studies show a 40% rise in reported mental health issues among adolescents over the past decade. The speaker also mentions that parents and schools are ill-equipped to counter this without policy intervention. In conclusion, the lecture highlights the importance of urgent regulatory frameworks to protect young users from harmful content.
What To Avoid
MISTAKES
✗
Starting with "I heard…" or "He said…" — use "The lecture discusses…" or "The speaker argues…".
✗
Using first person to paraphrase: "He said he thought climate was bad." — maintain academic reporting register.
✗
Trying to reproduce every detail — 4–5 key points delivered fluently beats 8 points delivered poorly.
✗
Going silent if you miss a point — say "the lecture also explores related aspects of this field" and continue.
✗
Stopping before 40 seconds — always fill the time even if you need to repeat the conclusion.
5–6 items3–9 sec audio10 sec to respondScores: Listening + Speaking (Core)

A short question plays — sometimes with an image. You answer with one word or a very short phrase. Questions test general world knowledge, vocabulary, and common facts. One correct word = full marks.

How To Attempt
1
Focus on the question word
What is…? / Who…? / What do you call…? The question word tells you exactly what type of answer is needed.
2
Answer immediately — within 1 second
The microphone window is short. Hesitation wastes recording time. Say your answer confidently.
3
One or two words maximum
Do not explain or justify. Extra words do not help — the AI scores on the single correct answer word.
4
Always attempt — never stay silent
A wrong answer scores zero — same as silence. But confident delivery at least keeps your fluency metrics intact.
Common Question Categories + Sample Answers
QUESTION TYPE
EXAMPLE → ANSWER
Opposites
"What is the opposite of expensive?" → cheap / inexpensive
Definitions
"What do you call a doctor who treats children?" → paediatrician
Geography
"What is the largest ocean?" → Pacific
Science
"What gas do plants absorb?" → carbon dioxide / CO₂
Tools
"What measures temperature?" → thermometer
Professions
"Who writes prescriptions?" → doctor / physician
Language
"Plural of child?" → children
Calendar
"Days in a leap year?" → 366
Preparation tip: Study a list of 200 common PTE ASQ answers — these questions repeat from a limited pool. Recognising the question instantly builds response speed and confidence significantly.
What To Avoid
MISTAKES
✗
Long answers: "I think it might be a thermometer because it measures temperature and…" — one word only.
✗
Saying "I don't know" or staying silent — always attempt an answer regardless.
✗
Over-thinking — ASQ answers are almost always the first word that comes to mind.
2–3 items (Academic)2–4 items (Core)10s prep · 40s speakScores: Speaking

You hear and read a real-world situation on screen. After audio ends you have 10 seconds to prepare (Academic) or 20 seconds (Core), then 40 seconds to speak. Respond naturally — as you would to a real person in that situation.

What The AI Scores
SUB-SKILL
WHAT IT MEASURES
Content
Does your response address all elements of the situation fully and appropriately?
Oral Fluency
Natural, conversational rhythm — not academic, not robotic
Pronunciation
Clear, intelligible delivery at a natural conversational pace
How To Attempt — Step By Step
1
During prep: identify 3 things
① WHO am I talking to? (neighbour, manager, customer) ② WHAT happened? ③ WHAT is my response? (apologise, request, explain, offer solution)
2
Open by acknowledging the situation naturally
Start with a natural opener relevant to the audience: "Hi, I just wanted to come over and…" / "Good morning, I'm calling about…"
3
State your main response clearly
Make your key point in one clear sentence. Then support it with a brief reason or explanation from the prompt details.
4
Offer a specific solution or next step
Strong responses always include a concrete action: "I'll make sure this doesn't happen again." / "Could we arrange a time to discuss this?"
5
Close politely and invite follow-up
End with: "Please let me know if there's anything else I can do." This signals communicative competence to the AI.
Universal 4-Part Template
OPENER — Address audience directly:
"[Hi / Hello / Good morning / Excuse me], I [wanted to reach out / am calling / came over] about [the situation from the prompt]."

MAIN RESPONSE — Your key action:
"I [sincerely apologise for / wanted to let you know that / would like to request / need to explain] [specific detail] because [brief reason]."

SOLUTION / NEXT STEP:
"I think the best way to [resolve / address / move forward] would be to [specific practical step], and I [will ensure / can arrange / would appreciate] [outcome]."

POLITE CLOSE:
"Please [let me know / feel free to contact me / don't hesitate to reach out] if you need anything further."
Situation Type Quick-Reference
SITUATION TYPE
KEY LANGUAGE
Apologising
I sincerely apologise for… · I take full responsibility… · It won't happen again.
Making a request
I was wondering if you could… · Would it be possible to… · I'd really appreciate it if…
Giving information
I wanted to let you know that… · Just to update you… · Please be aware that…
Solving a problem
The best approach would be to… · I suggest we… · One way to resolve this is…
Accepting / Declining
I'd be happy to… · Unfortunately, I'm unable to… · I'm afraid I won't be able to…
What To Avoid
MISTAKES
✗
Using academic/formal essay language — this is real-world conversation, use natural speech register.
✗
Ignoring one element of the prompt — every detail in the situation description must be addressed.
✗
Stopping before 40 seconds — add a polite follow-up or reiterate your solution to fill remaining time.
✗
Memorising one fixed response — situations vary, use the template structure not the exact words.
2–3 itemsAudio up to 3 min2 min to respondScores: Listening + Speaking

You listen to a 3-speaker conversation (up to 3 minutes) then have 2 minutes to summarise the main viewpoints. Double-skill task — scores both Listening AND Speaking simultaneously.

What The AI Scores
SUB-SKILL
WHAT IT MEASURES
Content
Did you accurately capture the key viewpoints all three speakers contributed?
Oral Fluency
Smooth, uninterrupted 2-minute delivery at natural pace
Pronunciation
Clear production maintained throughout the full response
Note-Taking System During Audio
WHAT TO WRITE ON YOUR NOTEPAD
▸
First 15 sec: Write the TOPIC — 1–2 words. Always established at the very start.
▸
Speaker A: Note their main position or key argument — 3–5 keywords.
▸
Speaker B: Do they agree, counter, or add a new angle? Note their key point.
▸
Speaker C: Note their contribution — especially if they propose a conclusion or solution.
▸
You do NOT need to track who said what — just capture 3 distinct viewpoints or ideas.
▸
Symbols: + = agrees · ✗ = disagrees · → = suggests · ? = question raised.
5-Sentence Response Template
① INTRODUCE TOPIC (8 sec):
"The group discussed [main topic], exploring a range of perspectives on [specific aspect]."

② FIRST VIEWPOINT (18 sec):
"One speaker argued that [Viewpoint A], emphasising that [key reason or supporting detail]."

③ SECOND VIEWPOINT (18 sec):
"Another participant [agreed / disagreed / extended this] by pointing out that [Viewpoint B], which [adds to / contrasts with the first view]."

④ THIRD VIEWPOINT (18 sec):
"A third perspective raised was that [Viewpoint C], suggesting that [implication or proposed solution]."

⑤ OVERALL CONCLUSION (10 sec):
"Overall, the discussion highlighted [central tension or shared conclusion] as a key issue requiring [further attention / a balanced approach]."
SAMPLE RESPONSE — Topic: Remote Work
The group discussed the growing trend of remote work, exploring its impact on productivity and workplace culture. One speaker argued that working from home dramatically improves work-life balance and reduces commuting stress, citing measurable output gains. Another participant disagreed, pointing out that remote work weakens team cohesion and makes onboarding new staff extremely difficult. A third perspective raised was that a hybrid model offers the most practical solution, preserving flexibility while maintaining social bonds. Overall, the discussion highlighted that while remote work brings genuine individual benefits, a balanced organisational policy is needed to address its wider professional drawbacks.
What To Avoid
MISTAKES
✗
Attributing specific quotes to specific speakers — summarise the ideas, not who said them.
✗
Only covering two of the three viewpoints — all three must feature for full Content marks.
✗
Adding your own personal opinion — this is a neutral summary of what the speakers discussed.
✗
Running out of time — 2 minutes = approximately 5 sentences at a natural pace. Plan your structure.
✗
Going silent if you miss a point — say "the discussion also explored related dimensions of this topic" as a bridge.
Training tip: Watch BBC HARDtalk, Al Jazeera panel discussions, or TEDx debates. After each one, summarise in 2 minutes out loud. This builds the exact listening-to-speaking conversion skill tested here.

Writing
2 task types · Part 1 · Templates, scoring & what to avoid
1–2 items10 min eachONE sentence: 5–75 wordsScores: Writing + Reading

A passage of up to 300 words appears. You must write a single summary sentence of 5–75 words in 10 minutes. Scores both Writing and Reading simultaneously — one of the highest-value tasks in the test.

What The AI Scores
SUB-SKILL
WHAT IT MEASURES
Content
Does the summary capture the MAIN idea of the passage accurately?
Form
Is it exactly ONE grammatically complete sentence of 5–75 words? (most heavily weighted)
Grammar
Are grammatical structures correct throughout the sentence?
Vocabulary
Is vocabulary appropriate, varied, and not simply copied from the passage?
CRITICAL — Form is the highest-weighted criterion: If you write more than one sentence (even separated by a semicolon), or fewer than 5 words, you score ZERO on Form. One complete sentence. Every time. No exceptions.
How To Attempt — Step By Step
1
Read quickly to find the main idea (2 min)
The main idea is almost always in the first and last paragraphs. Skim for the author's central claim — not a supporting example or minor detail.
2
Identify: Topic + What is said about it
Every good summary = Subject (the main topic) + Predicate (what the passage says about it). Find these two components before writing.
3
Draft your sentence using a complex structure (5 min)
Use a subordinate clause to connect the main argument to a supporting detail. Aim for 40–60 words — enough to show grammatical range.
4
Check: one sentence? 5–75 words? no copied phrases? (3 min)
Count your words. Verify there is no full stop creating a second sentence. Replace any phrase copied verbatim from the passage with a paraphrase.
4 Sentence Structure Templates
STRUCTURE 1 — Although / While contrast:
"Although [concession / opposing point from passage], the passage argues that [main claim], emphasising that [supporting detail]."

STRUCTURE 2 — The passage discusses:
"The passage discusses [topic], highlighting the fact that [main argument] and suggesting that [implication or recommendation]."

STRUCTURE 3 — Which relative clause:
"[Main topic], which [defining characteristic], is presented as [main claim] because [key reason from passage]."

STRUCTURE 4 — Causal structure:
"The passage argues that [main cause or problem] leads to [effect], and therefore recommends that [solution or implication]."
SAMPLE RESPONSE — Passage about deforestation (47 words)
"Although forests provide critical ecological services including carbon sequestration and biodiversity preservation, the passage argues that commercial deforestation driven by agricultural expansion is rapidly destroying these ecosystems, highlighting that without coordinated international policy intervention, irreversible environmental degradation will occur within the next two decades."
Useful Reporting Phrases
the passage argues the author contends emphasising that highlighting the importance of suggesting that which ultimately leads to despite the fact that as a consequence
What To Avoid
ZERO-SCORE MISTAKES
✗
Writing two sentences — even with a semicolon separating them, this risks a Form penalty.
✗
Fewer than 5 words — automatic zero on Form, the most important criterion.
✗
More than 75 words — automatic Form penalty. Count your words before submitting.
✗
Copying whole sentences from the passage verbatim — always paraphrase using synonyms.
✗
Summarising a minor detail instead of the MAIN idea — the first and last paragraphs hold the main argument.
✗
Using first person: "I think this passage is about…" — maintain formal academic register.
1–2 items20 min each200–300 wordsScores: All 6 Writing sub-skills

A topic is given. You write an argumentative essay of 200–300 words in 20 minutes. This task tests the widest range of writing sub-skills in the entire test.

What The AI Scores
SUB-SKILL
WHAT IT MEASURES
Content
Does the essay address the topic with relevant, developed ideas?
Form
Is it within 200–300 words? Under or over is penalised.
Grammar
Variety and accuracy — mix of simple + complex structures
Vocabulary
Range, precision, and appropriate academic register
Spelling
No errors. Choose British or American — be consistent throughout.
Written Discourse
Logical structure, paragraph cohesion, and smooth flow
How To Attempt — Step By Step
1
3 min — Plan on your notepad
Write 3 bullet points: ① Your position ② Main argument (Body 1) ③ Counter + rebuttal (Body 2). Never skip this — unplanned essays ramble and miss the word count.
2
14 min — Write in 4 clear paragraphs
Intro (~50w) → Body 1 (~80w) → Body 2 (~80w) → Conclusion (~40w). Each paragraph has ONE main idea.
3
3 min — Check grammar, spelling, word count
Read every sentence once. Fix one grammar structure if you spot it. Count words — aim for 240–270 for safety margin.
4-Paragraph Essay Template
INTRO (~50 words):
"In recent years, [topic/issue] has become a matter of considerable debate. While some argue that [opposing view], I firmly believe that [your clear position] for two key reasons."

BODY 1 — Main reason (~80 words):
"The principal reason for this view is that [reason 1]. This is because [explanation]. For instance, [specific example with data]. This demonstrates that [link back to thesis]."

BODY 2 — Counter + rebuttal (~80 words):
"Admittedly, some argue that [counter-argument]. However, this view overlooks the fact that [rebuttal]. While it is true that [concession], the evidence suggests that [reinforcement of your position]."

CONCLUSION (~40 words):
"In conclusion, it is evident that [restate main argument in different words]. [Governments / Individuals / Society] should [recommended action]. Only through [key solution] can [desired outcome] be achieved."
Grammar Structures To Include For 79+
USE AT LEAST 3 OF THESE IN YOUR ESSAY
▸
Passive voice: "It has been demonstrated that…" / "This is widely regarded as…"
▸
Conditional: "If governments fail to act, unemployment will rise significantly."
▸
Relative clause: "Workers, who lack digital skills, face the greatest risk."
▸
Inversion: "Not only does automation reduce costs, but it also increases output."
▸
Nominalisation: "The displacement of workers" not "when workers get displaced".
What To Avoid
SCORE-KILLERS
✗
Fewer than 200 words — automatic Form penalty. Count before submitting.
✗
More than 300 words — also penalised. Stop at 270–290 words for safety.
✗
Informal language: "gonna", "wanna", "kids", "a lot of" — always academic register.
✗
Repeating the same vocabulary — use synonyms: problem → issue / challenge / predicament.
✗

✗
Using general world knowledge to answer — the answer must come from what the speaker said.
✗
Selecting the most "detailed" or "elaborate" option — correct answers are usually clearly stated.
✗
Not reading options before audio — this is the most valuable preparation window available.
Master Listening Rule: Audio plays ONCE in every task — no replay, no pause. Your preparation time before each audio is your most valuable asset. Use every second to read options, questions, and transcripts. Never wait passively.
🔥 ViaTestPrep Tip
Read Aloud scores both Speaking and Reading simultaneously — the highest-value task in the test.
