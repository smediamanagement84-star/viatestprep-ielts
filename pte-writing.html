PTE Writing Mastery
1–2 items 10 min each ONE sentence: 5–75 words Scores: Writing + Reading High value task

A passage of up to 300 words appears. You must write a single summary sentence of 5–75 words in 10 minutes. Scores both Writing and Reading simultaneously — one of the highest-value tasks in the test.

CRITICAL WARNING — Form is Highest-Weighted
Form is the highest-weighted criterion
✗
If you write more than one sentence (even separated by a semicolon), or fewer than 5 words, or more than 75 words, you score ZERO on Form. One complete sentence only. No exceptions.
What The AI Scores
SUB-SKILL WHAT IT MEASURES
Content Does the summary capture the MAIN idea of the passage accurately?
Form Is it exactly ONE grammatically complete sentence of 5–75 words? (Zero marks if failed)
Grammar Are grammatical structures correct throughout the sentence? Mix of subordinate clauses.
Vocabulary Is vocabulary appropriate, academic, and not simply copied from the passage?
How To Attempt — Step By Step
1
Read quickly to find the main idea (2 min)
The main idea is almost always in the first and last paragraphs. Skim for the author's central claim — not a supporting example or minor detail.
2
Identify: Topic + What is said about it
Every good summary = Subject (the main topic) + Predicate (what the passage says about it). Find these two components before writing.
3
Draft your sentence using a complex structure (5 min)
Use a subordinate clause to connect the main argument to a supporting detail. Aim for 40–60 words — enough to show grammatical range.
4
Check: one sentence? 5–75 words? no copied phrases? (3 min)
Count your words. Verify there is no full stop creating a second sentence. Replace any phrase copied verbatim from the passage with a paraphrase.
4 Sentence Structure Blueprints
STRUCTURE 1 — Although / While contrast: "Although [concession / opposing point from passage], the passage argues that [main claim], emphasising that [supporting detail]." STRUCTURE 2 — The passage discusses: "The passage discusses [topic], highlighting the fact that [main argument] and suggesting that [implication or recommendation]." STRUCTURE 3 — Which relative clause: "[Main topic], which [defining characteristic], is presented as [main claim] because [key reason from passage]." STRUCTURE 4 — Causal structure: "The passage argues that [main cause or problem] leads to [effect], and therefore recommends that [solution or implication]."
SAMPLE RESPONSE — Passage about deforestation (47 words)
"Although forests provide critical ecological services including carbon sequestration and biodiversity preservation, the passage argues that commercial deforestation driven by agricultural expansion is rapidly destroying these ecosystems, highlighting that without coordinated international policy intervention, irreversible environmental degradation will occur within the next two decades."
Useful Reporting Phrases
the passage argues the author contends emphasising that highlighting the importance of suggesting that which ultimately leads to despite the fact that as a consequence
What To Avoid
MISTAKES TO AVOID
✗
Writing two sentences — even with a semicolon separating them, this risks a Form penalty.
✗
Fewer than 5 words or more than 75 words — automatic zero on Form.
✗
Copying whole sentences from the passage verbatim — always paraphrase using synonyms.
✗
Summarising a minor detail instead of the MAIN idea — look at the first and last paragraphs.
✗
Using first person: "I think this passage is about…" — maintain a formal academic register.
1–2 items 20 min each 200–300 words Scores: All 6 Writing sub-skills High value task

A topic is given. You write an argumentative essay of 200–300 words in 20 minutes. This task tests the widest range of writing sub-skills in the entire test, including grammar, vocabulary, spelling, and written discourse.

What The AI Scores
SUB-SKILL WHAT IT MEASURES
Content Does the essay address the topic with relevant, developed ideas?
Form Is it within 200–300 words? Under or over is heavily penalised.
Grammar Variety and accuracy — mix of simple, compound, and complex structures.
Vocabulary Range, precision, and appropriate academic register.
Spelling No errors. Choose British or American and stay consistent throughout.
Written Discourse Logical structure, paragraph cohesion, transition words, and smooth flow.
How To Attempt — Step By Step
1
3 min — Plan on your notepad
Write 3 bullet points: ① Your position ② Main argument (Body 1) ③ Counter + rebuttal (Body 2). Never skip this — unplanned essays ramble and miss the word count.
2
14 min — Write in 4 clear paragraphs
Intro (~50w) → Body 1 (~80w) → Body 2 (~80w) → Conclusion (~40w). Each paragraph has ONE main idea.
3
3 min — Check grammar, spelling, word count
Read every sentence once. Fix spelling or grammatical errors. Count words — aim for 240–270 for safety margin.
Universal 4-Paragraph Template
INTRO (~50 words): "In recent years, [topic/issue] has become a matter of considerable debate. While some argue that [opposing view], I firmly believe that [your clear position] for two key reasons." BODY 1 — Main reason (~80 words): "The principal reason for this view is that [reason 1]. This is because [explanation]. For instance, [specific example with data]. This demonstrates that [link back to thesis]." BODY 2 — Counter + rebuttal (~80 words): "Admittedly, some argue that [counter-argument]. However, this view overlooks the fact that [rebuttal]. While it is true that [concession], the evidence suggests that [reinforcement of your position]." CONCLUSION (~40 words): "In conclusion, it is evident that [restate main argument in different words]. [Governments / Individuals / Society] should [recommended action]. Only through [key solution] can [desired outcome] be achieved."
Grammar Structures To Include For 79+
USE AT LEAST 3 OF THESE IN YOUR ESSAY
▸
Passive voice: "It has been demonstrated that…" / "This is widely regarded as…"
▸
Conditionals: "If governments fail to act, unemployment will rise significantly."
▸
Relative clause: "Workers, who lack digital skills, face the greatest risk."
▸
Inversion: "Not only does automation reduce costs, but it also increases output."
▸
Nominalisation: "The displacement of workers" instead of "when workers get displaced".
What To Avoid
SCORE-KILLERS
✗
Fewer than 200 words or more than 300 words — automatic Form penalty. Stop at 240–280 words.
✗
Informal language: "gonna", "wanna", "kids", "a lot of" — always academic register.
✗
Repeating the same vocabulary — use synonyms: problem → issue / challenge / predicament.
✗
Only one body paragraph — always include at least two body paragraphs.
✗
Mixing British and American spelling in the same essay.
✗
Copying a memorised essay word-for-word — Pearson's AI detects and penalises template abuse. Customize slots heavily.

2–3 items 9 min each 80–120 words PTE Core only Scores: Writing (all sub-skills)

A prompt describes a real-world situation requiring an email. You write a formal or semi-formal email of 80–120 words in 9 minutes. Scored on email conventions (subject line, salutation, body, sign-off). PTE Core exclusive — does not appear in PTE Academic.

What The AI Scores
SUB-SKILL WHAT IT MEASURES
Content Did you address ALL points in the prompt? Missing one drops this score heavily.
Email Conventions Subject line · correct salutation · body paragraphs · appropriate sign-off. (Mandatory for full marks)
Form 80–120 words. Under or over is penalised.
Organisation Logical flow: opening purpose → body content → clear closing action.
Vocabulary & Grammar Appropriate register, correct formal phrasing, no contractions in formal emails.
Spelling Every word correctly spelled — consistent British or American English.
CRITICAL CONVENTION RULE
EMAIL CONVENTIONS ARE SCORING ANCHORS
✗
A missing subject line, wrong salutation, or missing sign-off will drop your conventions sub-score even if the body is excellent. Keep structural elements complete.
How To Attempt — Step By Step
1
Read the prompt and identify all 3 required points
Every prompt has exactly 3 elements you must cover. Underline them. Missing even one drops your Content score significantly.
2
Decide register: formal or semi-formal?
Email to a manager/company/institution = FORMAL (Dear Mr/Ms [Name], Yours sincerely). Email to a colleague/acquaintance = SEMI-FORMAL (Hi [Name], Kind regards). No contractions in formal emails.
3
Write Subject line first
Always include: "Subject: [clear summary of email purpose]". Scored under Email Conventions — missing it costs marks immediately.
4
Write in 3 body paragraphs
Para 1: State why you are writing. Para 2: Cover the 3 required points. Para 3: Close with a clear action or next step.
5
Count words and check spelling
Aim for 95–110 words — safely inside the 80–120 range. Re-read every sentence once for spelling errors.
Formal Email Template
Subject: [Clear, concise description of email purpose]

Dear [Mr/Ms Last Name] / Dear Sir or Madam,

PARA 1 — Purpose (1–2 sentences): "I am writing to [enquire about / apply for / inform you of / request] [specific subject from prompt]."

PARA 2 — 3 Required Points (3–4 sentences): "[Address Point 1 from the prompt]. Furthermore, [address Point 2]. I would also like to mention that [address Point 3]."

PARA 3 — Closing action (1–2 sentences): "I would be grateful if you could [specific action needed from recipient]. Please do not hesitate to contact me should you require any further information."

Yours sincerely,
[Your Full Name]
SAMPLE EMAIL — Band 79+ (~105 words)
Subject: Feedback on Recent Training Session

Dear Ms Johnson,

I am writing to share my feedback regarding the training session held last Tuesday. While I appreciated the effort involved, I found the content too general and not directly applicable to our day-to-day responsibilities, which limited its practical value.

I would suggest that future sessions focus on task-specific scenarios relevant to our team's workflow. Including interactive exercises and case studies would also significantly improve engagement and retention.

I would be grateful if a follow-up session could be arranged to address these areas more thoroughly. Please let me know if you would like to discuss this further.

Yours sincerely,
Alex Carter
What To Avoid
SCORE-KILLERS
✗
No subject line — automatic conventions deduction.
✗
No salutation or sign-off — "Dear..." and "Yours sincerely / Kind regards" are mandatory.
✗
Mixing formal and informal register or tone in the same email.
✗
Missing one of the 3 required content points — address every element in the prompt.
✗
Under 80 or over 120 words — count words before submitting.
✗
Contractions in formal emails: don't → do not · can't → cannot · I've → I have.
