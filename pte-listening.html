PTE Listening Mastery
1–2 items Audio 60–90 sec 10 min to write 50–70 words Scores: Listening + Writing

An audio recording of 60–90 seconds plays once. You write a 50–70 word summary in 10 minutes. Scores both Listening AND Writing simultaneously — the most complex task in the Listening section.

What The AI Scores
SUB-SKILL
WHAT IT MEASURES
Content
Does the summary capture the main point and key details accurately?
Form
Is it within 50–70 words? Multiple sentences are allowed.
Grammar
Grammatically correct sentences throughout
Vocabulary
Appropriate, varied, academic vocabulary — no copy-paste from audio
Spelling
Every word correctly spelled. Choose British or American consistently.
Note-Taking During Audio — Critical
WHAT TO WRITE ON YOUR NOTEPAD
▸
First 10 sec: Write the TOPIC — 1–2 words. Always established at the very start.
▸
Body: Key nouns, numbers, names, technical terms — hardest to reconstruct from memory.
▸
Last 10 sec: Note the conclusion or recommendation — speakers always signal it at the end.
▸
Shorthand only: → = leads to · + = also/and · ↑ = increase · ↓ = decrease · ? = problem raised.
▸
Do NOT try to write full sentences — your listening will suffer. Keywords only.
4-Sentence SST Template
SENTENCE 1 — Topic + Main Point: "The lecture discusses [main topic], with particular focus on [specific aspect]."

SENTENCE 2 — Central Argument / Finding: "The speaker argues that [central claim or finding], providing evidence that [supporting evidence or example]."

SENTENCE 3 — Additional Key Detail: "Furthermore, [second important point], which highlights [significance]."

SENTENCE 4 — Conclusion / Implication: "In conclusion, [key takeaway or recommendation from the speaker]."
SAMPLE SST RESPONSE (~62 words)
The lecture discusses the psychological effects of social media use on adolescents, with particular focus on self-esteem and sleep disruption. The speaker argues that excessive screen time correlates with increased anxiety and depression, citing studies showing a 40% rise in reported mental health issues. Furthermore, algorithmic design deliberately prolongs engagement, exacerbating these effects. In conclusion, urgent regulatory frameworks are needed to protect young users.
What To Avoid
SCORE-KILLERS
✗
Fewer than 50 words — automatic Form penalty. Count before submitting.
✗
More than 70 words — also penalised on Form. Always count during the final 60 seconds.
✗
Adding opinions not in the audio — Content is scored on accuracy to the source only.
✗
Copying exact phrases from the audio verbatim — paraphrase to demonstrate comprehension.
✗
Not counting words before submitting — this is the #1 cause of avoidable Form penalties.
2–3 items Audio plays once ⚠ NEGATIVE MARKING Scores: Listening

An audio recording plays once. A question with multiple answer options follows — more than one may be correct. Incorrect selections DEDUCT marks.

Negative marking rule: Correct selection = +1. Wrong selection = −1. Blank = 0. Never guess. Only select options you are genuinely confident were stated in the audio.
How To Attempt — Step By Step
1
Read ALL options before audio plays
Your prep time is the most valuable window. Reading options before the audio turns passive listening into an active search for specific information.
2
Listen for explicit statements matching options
Correct answers are usually stated directly in the audio. Wrong answers: not mentioned / contradict the speaker / only partially stated.
3
Apply the 80% confidence filter post-audio
Review each option: "Am I 80%+ confident this was clearly stated?" If uncertain, leave it blank — the deduction outweighs the potential gain.
Distractor Patterns
DISTRACTOR TYPE
HOW TO SPOT IT
Not mentioned
Plausible and topic-related but the speaker simply never said it
Partially true
Correct topic but wrong subject, quantity, or outcome stated
Too extreme
"always" / "all" when speaker said "often" / "most" / "some"
Opposite
Reverses the speaker's actual position or finding
What To Avoid
SCORE-KILLERS
✗
Selecting answers based on general knowledge — only what the speaker explicitly states counts.
✗
Selecting 3 or more options — rarely are more than 2 options correct. More selections = more risk.
✗
Not reading options before audio — you will not know what specific details to listen for.
2–3 items Audio plays once Spelling counts Scores: Listening + Writing

A transcript appears on screen with missing words. The audio plays simultaneously. You type the missing words exactly as heard. Spelling is marked — incorrect spelling = zero for that blank even if you heard the word correctly.

How To Attempt — Step By Step
1
Read the full transcript before audio starts
You usually have a few seconds before audio begins. Read the complete text to know where the blanks are and what words surround them — this primes your ears for exactly the right spot.
2
Follow the transcript word by word as audio plays
Track the audio with your eyes on the text. When you reach a blank, your ears are already primed. Blanked words are almost always content words (nouns, verbs, adjectives).
3
Type immediately while the word is fresh
Do not wait until audio ends to type. Type as you hear — short-term memory for specific words fades in 3–5 seconds. Type immediately and keep tracking.
4
Use context to confirm spelling after audio ends
Look at surrounding words — do they confirm the word class or spelling? Double-check each typed word before moving on. The text context almost always tells you if you got the right word.
Spelling Traps To Know
COMMON SPELLING ERRORS IN THIS TASK
▸
-tion vs -sion: education / communication / organisation — but: television / decision / conclusion.
▸
Double letters: necessary (1c 2s) · accommodation (2c 2m) · commitment (1t) · beginning (2g).
▸
British spellings: behaviour · colour · organisation · recognise · analyse (if using British English consistently).
▸
Homophones: affect/effect · their/there/they're · whose/who's — context determines which is correct.
Key difference: Unlike Highlight Incorrect Words, this task requires you to TYPE the missing words — spelling accuracy is scored directly. A correctly heard word spelled wrong = 0 marks for that blank.
What To Avoid
SCORE-KILLERS
✗
Writing phonetic spellings — the AI requires correct standard English spelling, not approximations.
✗
Leaving a blank empty — no negative marking, so always write your best attempt even if unsure.
✗
Looking away from the transcript during audio — lose your place once and you may miss multiple blanks.
2–3 items Audio 30–90 sec No negative marking Scores: Listening + Reading

An audio recording plays once. Afterwards, select which of 4–5 written paragraphs best summarises what you heard. Only one option is correct. Tests whether you understood the overall message — not specific details.

How To Attempt — Step By Step
1
Read ALL options before audio plays
Scan all 4–5 summary options during prep time. This gives you a clear map of differences to listen for — key words, different conclusions, contrasting claims. This is the single most impactful preparation move for this task.
2
Listen for the MAIN POINT — not details
This task tests overall understanding. Details are used in wrong options as distractors. The correct summary captures the central argument, not a specific supporting example.
3
Note the topic + conclusion during audio
The topic is stated in the first 10–15 seconds. The conclusion is signalled in the last 10 seconds with phrases like "in summary", "therefore" or "ultimately". These two anchor the correct summary option.
4
Eliminate wrong options systematically
Wrong options: too narrow (only one detail) / not mentioned / exaggerated / opposite meaning. The correct summary covers the FULL scope of the audio, not just one part of it.
Wrong Option Patterns
WRONG TYPE
HOW TO SPOT IT
Too narrow
Focuses on one specific detail from the middle of the audio but misses the main point
Not mentioned
Topic-related and plausible but the speaker simply never said it
Exaggerated
Uses "completely" / "all" / "never" when speaker used "often" / "tends to" / "may"
Opposite
Reverses the speaker's position — common trap for students who half-heard the argument
ACTIVE LISTENING STRATEGY
▸
Note the TOPIC (first 10 sec) and CONCLUSION (last 10 sec) — these two points anchor the correct summary.
▸
Ask: "What is the ONE main thing the speaker is saying?" — the correct option answers exactly this question.
▸
If two options seem similar, the correct one covers the FULL scope; the wrong one covers only part of the talk.
▸
Options using "only" or "exclusively" are almost always wrong — academic talks rarely deal in absolutes.
What To Avoid
MISTAKES
✗
Choosing the option with the most words matching what you heard — distractors deliberately echo the audio.
✗
Choosing based on general topic familiarity — the correct answer must match what THIS speaker specifically said.
✗
Not reading options before audio plays — you waste the most valuable preparation window you have.
2–3 items Audio 15–50 sec Partial negative marking per wrong click Scores: Listening + Reading

A written transcript appears while an audio recording plays. Some words in the transcript differ from what the speaker actually says. Click on every differing word. Scores both Listening AND Reading simultaneously.

Scoring rule: Correct word clicked = +1. Incorrect word clicked (a word that was actually said correctly) = −1. Word not clicked when it should be = 0. This task has negative marking per incorrect click.
How To Attempt — Step By Step
1
Use ALL prep time to read the full transcript
Read every word before audio starts. Familiarise yourself with the text so your brain detects deviations rather than processing both text and audio simultaneously from scratch.
2
Track the transcript word by word during audio
Follow the audio with your eyes on the text, word by word. The moment you hear a word that differs from what you are reading — click it immediately. Do not wait until audio ends.
3
Focus on content words — differences are always in nouns, verbs, adjectives
The AI never substitutes function words (the, a, of, and). Concentrate your listening effort on nouns, main verbs, and adjectives only — never function words.
4
If uncertain — do NOT click
Because of negative marking, uncertainty = leave it. A wrong click costs you more than a missed correct word. The safe choice is always to not click if unsure.
Typical Difference Patterns
TRANSCRIPT SAYS
SPEAKER ACTUALLY SAYS
large increase
significant increase (synonym substitution)
the government
the organisation (related word substitution)
economic growth
economic decline (antonym substitution)
researchers suggest
scientists suggest (synonym substitution)

Differences are always single-word substitutions — synonyms, antonyms, or related words. Never structural or grammatical changes.

What To Avoid
SCORE-KILLERS
✗
Clicking words because they "sound slightly different" due to accent — only click genuine word substitutions.
✗
Not reading the transcript before audio starts — the single most costly preparation mistake for this task.
✗
Trying to write down differences on notepad — you have no time; click in real time as you hear them.
✗
Clicking function words (the, a, of, and) — differences are never in function words.
3–4 items Audio plays once Scores: Listening + Writing Crucial task — every word counts

A short sentence — usually 7–13 words — is read aloud once. You type it exactly as heard. Spelling, capitalisation, and every word are scored. This task comes at the END of the test when fatigue is highest — do not let tiredness drop these marks.

What The AI Scores
CRITERION
DETAIL
Correct words
Each correct word in correct position = 1 point. Partial credit — each word scored independently.
Spelling
Misspelled word = 0 marks for that word even if correctly heard
Skills scored
Listening (recall accuracy) + Writing (spelling, grammar) simultaneously
The 3-Phase Method
1
LISTEN — Absorb the full sentence first
Do NOT start typing during the audio. Let the complete sentence play. Hold it in working memory. Focus especially on: the first word, the main verb, and the last word. These anchor the whole sentence structure.
2
NOTE — Write shorthand on notepad immediately after audio ends
Immediately after audio ends, scribble key anchor words: first 2 words + verb + last 2 words. This externalises your memory so you can type accurately without holding everything in your head.
3
TYPE — Carefully, checking as you go
Type from your notepad notes. Check: first word capitalised, all other words lower case (unless proper noun), correct spelling of each word. Re-read once before moving on.
Common WFD Sentence Patterns
Academic results / research findings: "The results of the study suggest that further research is required."
"Recent studies have demonstrated a clear link between diet and health outcomes."

Education / assignment: "Students are expected to submit their assignments before the deadline."
"The university provides a range of support services for international students."

Environment / science: "Environmental factors play a significant role in determining long-term outcomes."
"The development of renewable energy has accelerated significantly in recent years."

Government / policy: "The government has implemented a range of measures to address rising unemployment."
Spelling Traps
MOST COMMONLY MISSPELLED WORDS IN WFD
▸
-tion vs -sion: education · communication · organisation — but: television · decision · conclusion
▸
Double letters: necessary (1c, 2s) · accommodation (2c, 2m) · commitment (1t) · beginning (2g)
▸
Common academic words: environment · development · government · significant · demonstrate · implement
▸
British spellings: behaviour · colour · organisation · recognise (if using British consistently)
What To Avoid
SCORE-KILLERS
✗
Starting to type DURING the audio — you lose concentration on the second half of the sentence.
✗
Not re-reading what you typed — one missing word can cost 2–3 points on a 10-word sentence.
✗
Phonetic spelling: "their" as "there" / "affect" as "effect" / "principal" as "principle".
✗
Capitalising random words — only capitalise the first word and proper nouns.
✗
Giving up on a long sentence — type whatever you heard. Partial credit applies per correct word.
Practice tip: WFD sentences repeat from a known pool. Students who have practiced extensively will recognise sentences they have seen before. Regular dictation practice with academic English dramatically improves performance here.

2–3 items Audio 30–90 sec No negative marking Scores: Listening

An audio recording plays once. A question with 4–5 options follows — exactly one correct answer. No negative marking. The most straightforward listening task — always answer even if unsure.

How To Attempt — Step By Step
1
Read question AND all options before audio
Read the question carefully first: is it asking for main idea / specific detail / speaker's attitude / inference? Then read all options so you know what to listen for.
2
Skim for keywords from the options during audio
Each option has a key word or phrase. When you hear that keyword in the audio, pay close attention to whether the context matches the option's claim.
3
Eliminate using the 4 distractor patterns
Too extreme (always/never) · Partially correct · Not mentioned in audio · Opposite meaning. After elimination, the remaining option is correct.
Question Type Guide
QUESTION TYPE
WHERE TO FOCUS
Main idea
Listen to the opening statement and conclusion — these state the overall message
Specific detail
Listen for keywords from the question in the body of the recording
Speaker's attitude
Listen for evaluative language: "unfortunately" / "remarkably" / "it is clear that"
Inference
What does the speaker IMPLY but not directly state? Choose the logical conclusion.
No negative marking = always answer. Even a random guess between 2 remaining options is 50/50. Never leave MCQ Single blank.
What To Avoid
MISTAKES
✗
Answering based on general knowledge — the answer must come from the audio itself.
✗
Selecting the most "detailed" or "interesting" option — correct answers are usually clearly stated.
✗
Leaving blank — no negative marking, so always select your best option.
