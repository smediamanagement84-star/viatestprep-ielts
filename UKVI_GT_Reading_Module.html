Section A Strategy These texts are short — don't skim, read each one in full (they're 30–80 words). The questions ask for specific facts. Use keyword scanning. Watch for negatives ("does NOT include", "except"). Budget 14–16 minutes here.
Section B Strategy Workplace texts love lists, sub-headings, and bullet points. Use these as a map. Matching question types dominate here (matching information to source, matching headings). Budget 18–20 minutes.
Section C Strategy This is the closest to Academic. Use the full skim → scan → answer cycle. T/F/NG and MCQ are common here. Budget 22–26 minutes. This is where Band 7 is won or lost.

⏱ The 16–20–24 Timing Rule (GT version)

Section A 16 min · Short texts
Section B 20 min · Workplace
Section C 24 min · Article

Unlike Academic, GT gives you more texts upfront. Section A's brevity is deceptive — don't blow your time budget there.

The Changing Face of Apprenticeships in Britain

For much of the twentieth century, apprenticeships in Britain were associated almost exclusively with the traditional trades — plumbing, carpentry, electrical work. Young people who did not pursue academic routes often entered these programmes in their mid-teens, spending several years learning a craft under the supervision of an experienced tradesperson.

Since the introduction of the Apprenticeship Levy in 2017, the landscape has shifted considerably. The levy, which requires large employers to contribute 0.5% of their payroll to a dedicated fund, was designed to encourage businesses across all sectors to invest in apprenticeship training. As a result, apprenticeship programmes now exist in fields as varied as accountancy, digital marketing, nuclear engineering, and even law.

Critics, however, argue that quality has been sacrificed for quantity. A 2022 report by the Institute for Fiscal Studies found that many new apprenticeship schemes offer limited on-the-job learning, with apprentices spending disproportionate time on administrative tasks unrelated to their intended career. Completion rates have also declined, with fewer than half of all apprentices now finishing their programme.

Proponents counter that the expansion has opened doors for groups previously excluded from professional careers. Young people from lower-income backgrounds, in particular, now have structured pathways into industries that once recruited almost exclusively through graduate schemes.

🏢 SECTION B — WORKPLACE TEXT (Q4–6)

Grievance Procedure — Hartwell Manufacturing Ltd

Employees who have a workplace concern are encouraged to raise it informally with their line manager in the first instance. Most issues can be resolved at this stage without further escalation.

If the informal approach fails or is considered inappropriate (for example, where the concern involves the line manager directly), the employee may submit a formal written grievance to the HR department. The grievance must clearly state the nature of the complaint, the date(s) involved, and any witnesses or relevant documentation.

HR will acknowledge the grievance within five working days and arrange a formal hearing within 15 working days. The employee has the right to be accompanied by a colleague or trade union representative at this hearing. A written outcome will be provided within ten working days of the hearing.

If the employee remains dissatisfied, they may appeal within ten working days of receiving the written outcome. The appeal will be heard by a senior manager not previously involved in the case.

📰 SECTION C — EXTENDED ARTICLE (Q7–8)

The Rise of the Micro-Credential

In 2015, an unemployed software developer in Manchester completed a 12-week online course in data science and, shortly afterwards, secured a role at a financial technology firm. He had no university degree. His employer's primary concern was his demonstrated skill, not the institution that had taught him.

This story, once unusual, is now becoming commonplace. Across the UK, employers in sectors from construction to healthcare are increasingly recognising short, focused qualifications — known as micro-credentials — as valid evidence of workplace readiness. Unlike traditional degrees, which often take three or more years to complete, micro-credentials can typically be earned in weeks or months.

The shift has been accelerated by the pandemic. When millions of workers found themselves furloughed or redundant, many turned to online platforms to retrain. Government-backed schemes such as the Skills Bootcamps — funded through the National Skills Fund — further legitimised short-course qualifications in the eyes of both employers and regulatory bodies.

Not everyone is convinced. Critics from the higher education sector warn that micro-credentials lack the depth and critical thinking development that undergraduate programmes provide. There are also concerns about quality control — with hundreds of providers now offering certifications, standards can vary enormously. A badge from a well-regarded institution carries very different weight from one awarded by an unknown platform.

⑥ Your 7-Day Field Action Plan

DAY 1

DAY 2

Section B only — 2 workplace texts. Focus: Matching + T/F/NG on policy language

DAY 3

Section C only — 1 long article, timed 24 min. Focus: skim → scan → answer cycle

DAY 4

T/F/NG deep drill — 20 statements from mixed GT texts. Focus: FALSE vs NOT GIVEN distinction

DAY 5

Sentence completion drill — GT texts only. Focus: word limit discipline (max 3 words)
