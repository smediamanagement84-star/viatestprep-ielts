PTE Reading Tactics
5–6 items ~2 min each Scores: Reading + Writing Highest value reading task

A text with several blanks appears. Each blank has a dropdown with 4–6 word options. Select the best word for each blank. Tests vocabulary AND reading comprehension simultaneously — scores both Reading and Writing (vocabulary sub-skill).

What The AI Scores
CRITERION DETAIL
Correct selections Each correct blank = 1 point. Partial credit — each blank scored independently.
Skills contributed Feeds Reading score AND Writing (Vocabulary) score simultaneously.
How To Attempt — Step By Step
1
Read the full sentence around the blank BEFORE opening the dropdown
Never look at the options first. Read the complete sentence to understand meaning and identify the grammatical slot needed (noun / verb / adjective / adverb).
2
Identify the grammatical slot
Is the blank a noun (subject/object), verb (action), adjective (describes noun), or adverb (modifies verb)? This eliminates 2–3 options immediately.
3
Apply collocation logic
Words naturally pair together. "strong argument" not "powerful argument". "make a mistake" not "do a mistake". Knowing collocations often identifies the answer instantly.
4
Check register — academic texts require formal vocabulary
If one option is informal (big, good, bad) and another is formal (substantial, beneficial, detrimental), the academic text almost always requires the formal word.
5
Re-read the full sentence with your selected word
Does it sound grammatically correct and semantically natural? If yes, move on. If not, try the next best option.
4-Filter Elimination Method
FILTER 1 — Wrong word class: Eliminate any word that is the wrong part of speech for the slot (verb in adjective position, noun in adverb position).

FILTER 2 — Wrong collocation: Eliminate words that don't pair naturally with adjacent words. "carry out research" not "make research".

FILTER 3 — Wrong meaning or tone: Eliminate words with incorrect meaning or wrong formality level for the academic context.

FILTER 4 — Wrong preposition fit: Check prepositions around the blank. "responsible for" not "responsible of". "interested in" not "interested about".
Collocation bank to memorise
COMMON PAIRINGS
▸
carry out research · reach a conclusion · raise awareness
▸
draw attention to · make an impact · pose a threat
▸
meet the criteria · fulfil a requirement · bear a resemblance
What To Avoid
MISTAKES TO AVOID
✗
Opening the dropdown before reading the sentence — options are designed to distract.
✗
Spending more than 2.5 minutes on one item — move on and return if time allows.
✗
Randomly guessing without applying grammatical and collocation filters first.
✗
Ignoring register — informal words rarely appear as correct answers in academic texts.
2–3 items ~90 sec each ⚠ NEGATIVE MARKING Scores: Reading

A reading passage with a question. Multiple answers may be correct — typically 2 or 3 of 5–6 options. Incorrect selections DEDUCT marks. The only Reading task with negative marking.

Negative marking rule: Correct answer selected = +1. Incorrect answer selected = −1. Blank (not selected) = 0. Selecting a wrong answer is WORSE than leaving it blank. Only select options you are 80%+ confident about. If in doubt, select just ONE correct answer and move on.
How To Attempt — Step By Step
1
Read the question first — not the passage
Know exactly what you are looking for before reading. Is the question asking about: main idea / specific detail / writer's attitude / inference / vocabulary in context?
2
Skim passage for the relevant section only
Do not read every word. Locate the section relevant to the question and read only that part carefully.
3
Evaluate each option against the passage — not your knowledge
For each option ask: "Is this explicitly stated or clearly implied in the passage?" If neither, it is wrong — regardless of how plausible it sounds in general.
4
Apply the 80% confidence filter
Only select options you are 80%+ confident about. If you are 50/50 on an option, DO NOT select it — the deduction outweighs the potential gain.
Wrong Answer Patterns — What Distractors Do
DISTRACTOR TYPE HOW TO SPOT IT
Too extreme Uses absolute words like "always", "never", "completely" while the passage uses qualifiers like "often", "tends to", "may".
Partially true Mentions something from the passage but applies it to the wrong subject, time period, or context.
Not in text Sounds topic-related and highly plausible in the real world but is simply not stated or implied in the passage.
Opposite meaning Reverses the actual meaning of what the passage states (e.g. claims a positive link when it is negative).
What To Avoid
MISTAKES TO AVOID
✗
Selecting answers based on general world knowledge — only what the passage says counts.
✗
Selecting many options "to be safe" — this almost guarantees more deductions than gains.
✗
Spending more than 2 minutes — this task is worth fewer points than R&W Fill Blanks. Cap your time.
2–3 items ~2 min each Partial credit (adjacent pairs) Scores: Reading

Several text boxes appear in random order on the left. Drag and drop them into the correct sequence on the right to form a coherent paragraph. Partial credit is awarded — each correctly adjacent pair scores a point.

5-Step Re-ordering Method
1
Find the opening sentence first
The opening sentence introduces a NEW idea with NO pronoun references to anything before it. It does NOT start with "However", "This", "They", "It", "Furthermore". It may use the indefinite article "A" or "An" to introduce something for the first time.
2
Find the conclusion sentence
The closing sentence wraps up with "In conclusion", "Therefore", "Ultimately", "Thus", or provides a final outcome/implication that cannot logically be followed by another point.
3
Match pronouns to their antecedents
If a sentence starts with "He", "She", "It", "They", "This", "These" — the sentence INTRODUCING that person/thing/concept must come immediately before it.
4
Follow logical connectors in sequence
"Firstly" → "Secondly" → "Finally". "For example" comes after a general claim. "However" comes after a positive statement. "Furthermore" adds to the same argument.
5
Read the full sequence in your head
After placing all boxes, read the full paragraph mentally. Does it flow? Is there a sudden topic jump? If so, swap the two boxes either side of that jump.
Connector Sequence Guide
SIGNALS AN OPENING SENTENCE: No pronouns referencing prior content · Introduces a new topic with "A/An" · No linking word at the start

SIGNALS A FOLLOWING SENTENCE: Starts with "This" / "These" / "It" / "They" / "He" / "She" · Starts with "However" / "Furthermore" / "Additionally" · Uses "The [thing already introduced]"

SIGNALS A FINAL SENTENCE: "In conclusion" / "Therefore" / "Thus" / "Ultimately" / "In summary" · Contains a recommendation, result, or outcome · No idea that requires further development
Partial credit tip: Even if you cannot determine the full order, getting the FIRST and LAST sentences correct is worth points and is usually the most identifiable step. Build from the outside in. Scored per correct adjacent pair.

4–5 items ~90 sec each Partial credit · No negative marking Scores: Reading

A text with several blanks appears. A word pool at the bottom contains more words than blanks — not all will be used. Drag words from the pool into the correct blank positions. Tests vocabulary in context and reading comprehension.

How To Attempt — Step By Step
1
Read the whole text before touching the word pool
Get a sense of the topic and tone before looking at options. This primes your vocabulary matching.
2
Identify the word class needed for each blank
Is the blank a noun? verb? adjective? adverb? Check what comes before and after: "a [blank] solution" needs an adjective; "[blank] the problem" needs a verb.
3
Filter the word pool by word class first
Eliminate words from the pool that are the wrong part of speech. Usually 2–3 words will fit the class — then use meaning and collocation to select the correct one.
4
Fill the easiest blanks first
Once you place a word it disappears from the pool, narrowing choices for remaining blanks. Fill confident choices first to gain this process-of-elimination advantage.
5
Use context clues — synonyms, antonyms, examples nearby
Often the text provides a clue. "Unlike X, the [blank] approach is…" — the blank likely means the opposite of X.
Key difference from R&W FiB: Words are dragged from a shared pool — not dropdowns. Each word can only be used once. Process of elimination becomes more powerful as you place each word.
What To Avoid
MISTAKES TO AVOID
✗
Starting with the word pool before reading the text — you will be matching words without context.
✗
Forcing a word that fits grammatically but not semantically — both must be correct.
✗
Leaving blanks empty — no negative marking, so always make a best-guess selection.
2–3 items ~60–90 sec each No negative marking Scores: Reading

A passage and question with 4–5 options. Exactly ONE answer is correct. No negative marking — always select an answer even if unsure. Tests main idea, specific detail, inference, or writer's purpose.

How To Attempt — Step By Step
1
Read the question — not the options — first
Know whether you're looking for: main idea / specific fact / writer's opinion / inference / vocabulary in context. This focuses your reading.
2
Locate the answer zone in the passage
For specific details: skim for the keyword from the question. For main idea: focus on the first and last sentences of each paragraph.
3
Eliminate wrong answers using 4 distractor patterns
Too extreme (always/never) · Partially correct · Not in text · Opposite meaning. Eliminate until one option remains.
Question Type Guide
QUESTION TYPE WHERE TO LOOK
Main idea First sentence of passage + conclusion paragraph.
Specific detail Skim for keywords from the question in the body of the text.
Writer's attitude Look for evaluative language: "unfortunately", "remarkably", "it is clear that".
Inference What does the text IMPLY but not directly state? Choose the logical conclusion from given info only.
Vocabulary in context Re-read the full sentence — which option best fits the meaning in context?
No negative marking = always answer. Even a random guess between 2 remaining options is 50/50. Never leave MCQ Single blank.
Time Management — Reading Section: R&W Fill Blanks ~2 min per item · MCQ Multiple ~90 sec · Re-order Paragraphs ~2 min · Reading FiB ~90 sec · MCQ Single ~60 sec. Total: ~28 min. The timer is shared — monitor it constantly.
