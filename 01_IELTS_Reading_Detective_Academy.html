② The Detective's Toolkit

Two skills. That's it. Every other strategy is built on these.

🗺

Skim — Build the Map

2–3 minutes max

Strategic glancing. Read the title, the first paragraph carefully, the first sentence of every other paragraph, and the last paragraph. Goal: a mental map of where topics live.

Goal: "Para A = history. Para B = problems. Para C = a Japanese study." Now you know where to hunt.
🔦

Scan — The Searchlight

Hunt for a keyword

Let your eyes fly across the text, ignoring sentences, until you hit a keyword (or its synonym). When you find it — stop. Read carefully around it.

Best keywords: proper nouns, dates, numbers, technical terms.

③ The Game Plan

Step 1 · 60 sec
Preview Questions

Identify question types, not details. Note easy keywords (dates, names).

Law of the Clock

The 18–20–22 Rule

Passages get harder. Don't spend equal time on each. Aim for 17–18 min on P1, 20 min on P2, 22–23 min on P3.

The Lost Library of Sutton

For more than a century, the small market town of Sutton in northern England was famous for an unlikely treasure: a private library assembled by the eccentric collector Hugh Marlow between 1893 and 1925. The collection, eventually numbering over 12,000 volumes, was renowned for its medieval manuscripts and early scientific texts.

The library's fame was matched by its inaccessibility. Marlow rarely allowed visitors and refused to lend any item to outside scholars. After his death in 1928, the collection passed to a local trust, which opened the building to the public for limited hours but did little to catalogue or conserve its contents.

By the late 1970s, water damage and theft had taken a toll. A 1981 audit, led by the historian Dr. Eleanor Ash, revealed that as many as 3,000 volumes were missing or damaged beyond repair. Critics blamed inadequate funding, while the trust argued the building's location had always made conservation difficult.

In 2002, a digitisation project began. By 2018 over 8,000 volumes had been scanned and made publicly available online. The lead archivist credited the project's success to the diversity of expertise on the team rather than to any single innovation.
